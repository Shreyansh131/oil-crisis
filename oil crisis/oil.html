OIL CRISIS

To know oil crisis, we have to understand. The meaning of crude oil and how it affects the world economy?

Crude oil is a raw natural resourse which is a mixture of hydrocarbon that exists in comparitively volatile liquid phase in natural underground reservoirs Also, known as "Black Gold". It known as black gold due to its high commercials value, it has the potentials which can change the scale of global economics. Rise or fall on crude oil prices, its impact goes to its by-product such as cooking oils, petrol/diesel, paints, tyres, tranportation and many more.

In present time, without crude oil and its by-product, maintain a balance economic order is very difficult.

But what makes global oil prices to go up or down?
oil prices depends on many factor such as
  • Supply and Demand
  • Opec and other organisation
  • Spot and Futures Markets
  • Geopolitical conditions
Geopolitical events or natural diaster disturb the supply and demand in the world which directly affects the price of crude oil globally.
Slightly change in crude oil price can trigger inflation, recession or an energy crisis because oil and gas are backbone of global trade and plays a crucial role in development and prosperity of a country.

  1. Abadan crisis or Iran oil crisis (1951-1954):

  2. 1973 Oil Crisis:
    At the time of Arab-Isralei war (October 1973), Arab members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) imposed sanctions against the United States in retaliation for U.S. production. the decision to resupply the Israeli army to reward and strengthen the post-war peace negotiations.
    The economic impact of 1973 oil crisis:
    70% raised in October 1973 to $5.11 per barrel, while many members also began cutting production by 5% per month. In Dec 1973, the price of crude oil raised $7 per barrel. It rose four-folds to nearly $12 per barrel by March 1974, when the embargo was lifted. The United States, a major oil consumer, was badly hit by the embargo, as fuel prices rose from an average of 39 cents per gallon to 53 cents per gallon by 1974. The oil embargo came at a wrong time for the world's largest economy.

  3. 1979 energy crisis:
    In 1979, Oil production drop due to Iranian revolution which led to energy crisis. THE UNITED STATES experienced the 2nd oil crisis in 1970s. American consumers were told that the cause of the crisis was a decline in Iranian oil production from 5.8 million barrels a day (mmbd) in July 1978 to 445,000 barrels a day (mbd) in January 1979. The short-run consequences of the crisis were shortages of diesel fuel and gasoline during the months of May, June, and July.

  4. 1990s oil price shock:
    The 1990 oil crisis was a major event that not only affected the global oil market and economy at the time, but also had lasting effects on energy policy and international relations. Oil crisis was primarily triggered by Iraq's invasion of Kuwait on August 2, 1990. This attack halted oil production from Kuwait and raised fears of potential disruption from other countries in the region. Prices nearly doubled from $15 per barrel in July 1990 to about $40 per barrel by mid-October 1990.The sharp rise in oil prices led to inflation and recession in many countries, especially those heavily dependent on oil imports.

  5. 2020 oil crisis:
    In 2020, due to COVID-19 pandemic oil and gas demand drop dramatically at global level. Lockdowns and travel restrictions paused transportation. As storage facilities filled up, there is oversupply crisis. Major oil-producing countries like Saudi Arabia and Russia engaged in a price war, exacerbating the situation. The crisis severely impacted oil-dependent economies, strained energy markets, and prompted emergency production cuts by OPEC+ to stabilise prices.
